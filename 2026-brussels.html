Day 1 — Saturday, July 11: Meuse Cliffs

Brussels → train to Namur → Meuse RAVeL south → Maredsous Abbey → Dinant (~45km, flat)

📷 Photo Opportunities

  • Namur Citadel: Cross to the Jambes side of the Meuse for the classic framing — citadel reflected in the river with the city below. Early morning gives soft light and no crowds.
  • Meuse RAVeL cliffs: The limestone walls are tallest between Wépion and Dinant; July afternoon light hits the west-facing cliffs from about 3–5pm. Look for hang-gliders launching from the Sept Meuses cliffs.
  • Annevoie gardens: Overcast days are ideal — flat light eliminates harsh shadows on the water features and reduces glare on the reflecting pools. The buffet d’eau cascade films well in slow shutter.
  • Maredsous Abbey: The abbey courtyard with a Maredsous beer in hand is an obvious shot — but the real prize is the exterior facade at golden hour (roughly 8–9pm in July), when the stone turns amber.
  • Dinant at dusk: Stay for blue hour — the church dome and citadel light up, and the Meuse reflects the full scene. The best angle is from the bridge or the opposite bank. Arrive 30 minutes before sunset to secure a spot.

Morning

  • Train from Brussels-Midi to Namur (~1 hour). SNCB direct trains run frequently.
  • Pick up rental bike in Namur or assemble your own at the station
  • Namur Citadel — Vauban-designed fortress on a rocky promontory above the Meuse-Sambre confluence. Panoramic views across the city. The largest medieval fortress in Belgium.

Afternoon

  • Join the Meuse RAVeL (EuroVelo 19) heading south — car-free asphalt path along the riverbank, limestone cliffs rising on both sides
  • Wépion — Strawberry village on the Meuse; strawberry museum and farm stands in season
  • Sept Meuses viewpoint — Hang-gliders launch from the cliffs here; dramatic river panorama
  • Jardins d’Annevoie — 18th-century water gardens powered entirely by gravity. One of the most visited gardens in Belgium. Allow 1 hour.
  • Château de Poilvache — Ruined 13th-century castle on a cliff above the river

Late Afternoon

  • Detour up the Moligné Valley — RAVeL Line 150A through dense forest (~8km each way)
  • Maredsous Abbey — Neo-Gothic Benedictine monastery built into the hillside. Famous for its beer (Maredsous 6, 8, 10) and cheese, both brewed and produced on-site. The abbey shop sells directly — have a beer in the courtyard.
  • Return to the Meuse and continue south to Dinant

Evening

  • Arrive Dinant — Belgium’s most photographed view: onion-domed collegiate church, cliff-top citadel, and the Meuse reflected below
  • Visit the Dinant Citadel (cable car available) for sunset views
  • Adolphe Sax birthplace — The inventor of the saxophone was born here; small museum on the main street
  • Dinner in Dinant — Leffe is brewed just upstream; widely available on tap

Day 2 — Sunday, July 12: Into the Ardennes

Dinant → Fonds de Leffe → Rochefort → Marche-en-Famenne → La Roche-en-Ardenne (~65km, rolling)

Abbaye Saint-Rémy, Rochefort

La Roche-en-Ardenne castle & Ourthe

Ourthe valley from La Roche aux Faucons, Belgian Ardennes

La Roche-en-Ardenne — Ourthe meander looking upstream

📷 Photo Opportunities

  • Fonds de Leffe valley: Early morning mist settles in the narrow valley floor in July — if you leave Dinant by 7am you may catch it. The beech forest and stream make strong foreground elements.
  • Condroz villages: The limestone farmhouses of Thynes and Sovet are photogenic in warm afternoon light — look for the typical Condroz style (blue-grey stone, red tile roofs) against the rolling green hills.
  • Rochefort Abbey: The abbey exterior is freely photographable from the road. The austere Cistercian facade photographs well in flat overcast light. A glass of Rochefort 10 on a café table is a classic travel shot.
  • La Roche-en-Ardenne: Climb up to the castle ruins at sunset — you get an elevated view straight down the Ourthe meander with the town below. The ruined towers frame the valley nicely. Golden hour in July falls around 9pm.
  • La Roche from below: The view upstream from the main bridge, with the castle silhouetted against the wooded hillside, is the classic La Roche postcard shot.

Morning

  • Depart Dinant east on EuroVelo 5 (Via Romea Francigena, voted Cycle Route of the Year 2023)
  • Fonds de Leffe — Nature reserve in the valley of the Leffe stream, where Leffe beer has been brewed since 1152
  • Condroz plateau — limestone villages (Thynes, Sovet), quiet country roads through farmland transitioning to forest
  • Ciney — Small market town, good for a mid-morning coffee stop

Afternoon

  • Rochefort — One of only 14 authentic Trappist breweries in the world. The Abbaye Notre-Dame de Saint-Rémy produces Rochefort 6, 8, and 10. The 10 (11.3% ABV) is considered among the world’s finest beers. The brewery is technically closed to visitors but the abbey church is open and the local cafés all serve Rochefort on tap.
  • Grottes de Han (optional) — Famous cave system near Han-sur-Lesse, accessible by tram from Rochefort area
  • Marche-en-Famenne — Transition point from Condroz to Famenne to Ardennes. Terrain gets hillier and more forested from here.

Evening

  • Hotton — Pick up the Ourthe river valley; the terrain becomes noticeably more dramatic
  • Durbuy (optional, ~5km detour off route) — Officially recognized as the smallest city in the world, Durbuy is a medieval walled town perched above the Ourthe. The cobbled lanes, 13th-century castle, and overhanging rock face above the river make it one of the most photogenic towns in Belgium. Adds ~10km and 45 minutes.
  • Arrive La Roche-en-Ardenne — The Ardennes cycling hub. A small town in a dramatic Ourthe river meander, surrounded by dense forest on all sides, with a ruined 9th-century castle on the hill above.
  • Dinner in La Roche — local trout and Ardennes ham are regional specialties

Day 3 — Monday, July 13: Ourthe Valley & Bastogne

La Roche → Hérou viewpoint → Houffalize → Bastogne → RAVeL 163 → Gouvy (~70km, hilly — biggest day)

Hérou belvedere — Ourthe meanders

Mardasson Memorial, Bastogne

Ourthe Orientale valley, Houffalize — classic Ardennes forest

Ardennes forest trail, Parc naturel des Deux Ourthes

📷 Photo Opportunities

  • Ourthe Valley riding shots: The dense forest creates natural light tunnels along the river — shooting along the path with the river on one side and forest canopy above works especially well in dappled morning light. Polarising filter recommended to cut reflections off the water.
  • Hérou viewpoint: This is the signature shot of the entire trip. Arrive early morning (before 9am) for mist in the valley — the Ourthe loops emerge from cloud like something from a Chinese landscape painting. Wide-angle lens essential. Allow time: the hike up takes 10–15 minutes from the road.
  • Houffalize: The confluence of the two Ourthe branches makes a nice aerial-style shot from the hilltop above town. The rebuilt church spire above the valley is photogenic at any time of day.
  • Mardasson Memorial: The star shape is only apparent from above (drone, or the viewing terrace on the memorial itself). At ground level, shoot through the archways framing the Ardennes landscape. The memorial has intentional sightlines — walk the full perimeter. Evening light from the west catches the stone well.
  • RAVeL 163 viaducts: The old railway viaducts carrying the converted path through the Ardennes are dramatic structures — position yourself below and use a wide angle to capture the arches against the forest and sky.

Morning

  • Head south along the Ourthe Valley — the most famous cycling section in the Ardennes. Dense oak forest, wild river meanders, almost no traffic. This is what people mean when they say “Ardennes cycling.”
  • Nadrin / Hérou viewpoint — A 10-minute hike from the road (or you can see it from the road on a clear day) reaches one of the most dramatic panoramas in Belgium: the Ourthe loops in seven visible meanders far below, surrounded by unbroken forest. Worth the detour.
  • Houffalize — Confluence town where two Ourthe branches meet. Hit hard during the Battle of the Bulge (December 1944 – January 1945); the rebuilt town center has a memorial and photos showing the destruction.

Afternoon

  • South to Bastogne — The city that held out during the Battle of the Bulge. General McAuliffe’s one-word reply to German surrender demands (“Nuts!”) was issued here.
  • Bastogne War Museum — Excellent and moving WWII museum with immersive exhibits. Allow 2 hours.
  • Mardasson Memorial — Star-shaped American memorial on a hill outside town; panoramic views over the Ardennes landscape where the battle was fought
  • Giant cyclist statue — Bastogne marks the turnaround point of Liège–Bastogne–Liège, one of cycling’s five monuments. A large bronze cyclist stands in the town center.

Evening

  • Pick up RAVeL 163 east from Bastogne — cited as one of the most beautiful trails in the entire RAVeL network. A former railway line converted to cycling path, running through deep Ardennes forest with gentle gradients (max 2%), viaducts, and cuttings.
  • Arrive Gouvy — Small village near the Luxembourg border, gateway to the Vennbahn. The end of the Ardennes section.

Day 4 — Tuesday, July 14: Vennbahn & High Fens

Gouvy → Burg-Reuland → Sankt Vith → High Fens → Monschau (~70km, gentle rail trail)

High Fens (Hautes Fagnes) — wooden boardwalk across Brackvenn bog

Monschau old town in the Rur gorge

📷 Photo Opportunities

  • Vianden castle (optional excursion): Take the chairlift above the town for an elevated view straight down onto the castle, village, and Our river valley — this is the definitive Vianden shot and one of the strongest images of the whole trip. From below, the bridge over the Our with the castle rising directly behind is the classic postcard angle. Late morning light from the east catches the castle’s main facade well.
  • Burg-Reuland castle: The 10th-century ruin sits on a rocky promontory above the Our river. Best shot is from the meadow below, looking up at the tower with the forested hills behind — morning light from the east catches the facade. The river reflection in calm conditions is exceptional.
  • Vennbahn leading lines: The rail trail is dead straight for long stretches — the converted path disappearing into forest makes a classic leading-lines composition. Try lying the bike in the frame for scale.
  • High Fens wooden boardwalks: The long wooden walkways across the bog are a distinctive visual. Shoot along the boardwalk at low angle in early morning or evening light — the peat and cotton grass glow warm amber. In July the heather may be starting to purple (peaks August).
  • Signal de Botrange: Wide open plateau — good for big-sky shots and the monument silhouette at sunset. At 694m, you get above the cloud inversions that form in the valleys.
  • Monschau from above: The most dramatic Monschau shots are from the hillside paths above the town looking down into the gorge — the half-timbered roofline against the forest. Reach the viewpoint via a 15-minute walk up from the Rotes Haus. Early morning before the day-trippers arrive (Monschau gets very busy by 10am in summer).
  • Monschau Rur reflection: At river level, shoot the half-timbered facades reflected in the still Rur water — best in early morning before wind picks up. The narrow footbridges over the river are good foreground elements.

Morning — Optional Vianden Excursion

  • Vianden, Luxembourg (optional half-day excursion using free public transport) — Luxembourg’s most-visited attraction and one of the largest fortified castles west of the Rhine, with 10th-century origins. The castle towers above the Our river on a dramatic rocky ridge; the medieval town below is compact and well-preserved. Victor Hugo lived here in exile and his house is now a museum. Not a country-tick stop — a real destination.
  • How to get there without adding 100km: Overnight in Troisvierges (~10km from Gouvy, just over the Luxembourg border) and leave the bikes at the accommodation. Luxembourg has completely free public transport — take the train south to Clervaux (~15 min), Clervaux→Ettelbruck (~30 min), then bus 181 to Vianden (~25 min). Allow 2 hours at the castle. Reverse the journey and you’re back in Troisvierges by early afternoon. Alternatively, a taxi from Troisvierges to Vianden is ~45km each way (~35–45 min); share the cost between two people and it’s comparable to a train fare — and far simpler with no connections or timetable to juggle.
  • Trade-off: You start the Vennbahn around 1pm instead of 9am. The Vennbahn is a gentle rail trail (max 2% gradient) so afternoon riding is entirely manageable.
  • Connect to the Vennbahn heading north — Europe’s longest cross-border rail trail (125km), built on a former Prussian military railway from 1889. Awarded Cycling Route of the Year 2014. Max gradient 2%, smooth surface throughout.
  • Burg-Reuland — Picturesque Our valley village with a 10th-century castle on a promontory above the river. The nearby Lengeler tunnel has been converted into a bat habitat with interpretive boards — a memorable passage.
  • Sankt Vith — Capital of the German-speaking community of Belgium. Vennbahn Museum in the old station building tells the history of the railway and the bizarre Treaty of Versailles corridor that transferred this territory from Germany to Belgium in 1919.

Afternoon

  • Continue north through Ardennes farmland — Born (historic railway viaduct), Faymonville, Sourbrodt (former Vennbahn hub with historical exhibits)
  • Signal de Botrange — Belgium’s highest point at 694m, on the edge of the High Fens. A monument and observation tower mark the summit.
  • High Fens – Eifel Nature Park — Wild peatland moorland with wooden boardwalks across the bog. This landscape feels completely unlike anything else in Belgium — more like the Scottish Highlands or Scandinavian fell. Protected since 1971 as Belgium’s largest nature reserve (700km²).

Evening

  • Mützenich — The Vennbahn passes through a bizarre Treaty of Versailles corridor: a strip of Belgian territory surrounded on all sides by Germany. Cycling between German enclaves on Belgian railway land.
  • Arrive Monschau — A fairy-tale half-timbered German town squeezed into a dramatic Rur river gorge. Rated 4.7 stars with over 9,000 reviews; regularly listed among Germany’s most beautiful small towns. Narrow cobbled lanes, mustard-yellow timber-framed buildings, the 17th-century Red House (Rotes Haus).
  • Dinner in Monschau — try local Eifel cuisine; Monschauer Senf (mustard) is a specialty

Monschau → Roetgen → Raeren → Kornelimünster → Aachen (~25km, easy morning)

RAVeL on old Vennbahn trackbed near Ondenval — forested rail trail

📷 Photo Opportunities

  • Monschau morning: Before you leave — the Rur river at first light (sunrise is around 5:30am in mid-July). The town is deserted, the water is still, and the timber-framed facades reflect perfectly. This is the best light of the trip.
  • Raeren pottery: The stoneware pieces in the museum and local shops make fine close-up still-life shots — the grey-blue glaze photographed against stone is distinctive.
  • Aachen Cathedral exterior: The octagonal tower photographs well from the Domhof square to the south, with the Gothic choir to the right. Midday light works here since the building faces multiple directions — the original Carolingian rotunda faces south.
  • Aachen Cathedral interior: The golden mosaics in the Palatine Chapel dome, the Byzantine-inspired arcade, and Charlemagne’s chandelier (a 12th-century gift from Frederick Barbarossa) are extraordinary subjects. Tripod is technically not permitted but a steady hand at ISO 3200 works. The best interior light is in the afternoon when sun enters the west windows.
  • Cathedral treasury: Photography is allowed in the treasury. The Lothar Cross (990 AD) and the bust reliquary of Charlemagne are extraordinary close-up subjects — bring a macro lens or use portrait mode.
  • Aachen Marktplatz: The Gothic town hall and the fountain of Charlemagne in the market square make a classic composition. Evening light from the west catches the town hall facade after 7pm.

Morning

  • Short morning stroll through Monschau before departing — explore the old town lanes, visit the Red House (Rotes Haus) museum if open
  • Vennbahn north, mostly gentle downhill toward Aachen
  • Roetgen — Edge of the Eifel; transition into the suburbs of Aachen
  • Raeren — Former Vennbahn station, now the Raeren Pottery Museum — the village has been producing stoneware since the 16th century; the local “Raeren grey” glaze is distinctive

Afternoon

  • Kornelimünster — Charming former abbey town on the edge of Aachen, half-timbered buildings and a Carolingian-era monastery
  • Arrive Aachen (Aix-la-Chapelle) — Charlemagne’s 8th-century capital
  • Aachen Cathedral — UNESCO World Heritage Site #1 (listed 1978, the first site in Germany). Charlemagne’s octagonal Palatine Chapel, built 796–805, is the centerpiece. The Gothic choir was added in the 14th century. Interior mosaics are original Byzantine-inspired work from the 9th century. Charlemagne’s throne is in the upper gallery. €7 guided tour strongly recommended — the treasury holds the reliquary of Charlemagne, the bust reliquary with his skull, and the Lothar Cross.
  • Cathedral Treasury — Considered one of the most important collections of medieval ecclesiastical art in Europe
  • Explore the old town — the market square (Marktplatz), the historic Elisenbrunnen (sulphur springs pavilion), the narrow lanes of the Pontstrasse
  • Aachener Printen — The city’s famous spiced gingerbread, protected geographical indication. Pick up a box from Leo Driessen or Nobis Printen as a souvenir.

Evening / Departure

  • Train options from Aachen Hauptbahnhof:
    • Aachen → Brussels (~2h via Liège, Thalys/IC)
    • Aachen → Cologne (~1h, ICE/IC) — connect onward across Europe

Notes & Logistics

Getting There & Away

  • Fly into Brussels (BRU) — train from the airport to Brussels-Midi takes ~20 minutes
  • Brussels-Midi → Namur ~1 hour on SNCB intercity trains; runs frequently throughout the day
  • Return: Aachen → Brussels ~2 hours via Liège on Thalys or IC. Alternatively, Aachen → Cologne (~1h) for onwards travel.

Bikes on Trains

  • SNCB (Belgian rail) allows bikes on most intercity trains with a “bike card” (€5 single journey) or “bike day pass” (€8, unlimited trains all day, valid across whole country)
  • Bikes must be foldable or unboxed; check the bicycle symbol on the train indicator boards for which carriages accept bikes
  • The Aachen–Brussels train (Thalys) does not accept non-folding bikes; plan to return via Liège intercity instead, or box the bike

Bike Rental

  • Rental available in Namur (several shops near the station) and Dinant
  • Many Bed&Bike accommodations along the Vennbahn rent or store bikes
  • For the full route, bringing your own or renting in Namur and returning in Aachen is most flexible

Accommodation

  • 50+ Bed&Bike certified accommodations along the Vennbahn alone — these have secure bike storage, tools, and drying rooms. Search vennbahn.eu.
  • La Roche-en-Ardenne has a wide range of hotels, B&Bs, and hostels — book ahead in summer
  • Gouvy is small; options are limited. Consider staying in Vielsalm (~8km south of Gouvy) which has more choices.
  • Monschau fills up on weekends; book accommodation at least 2 weeks ahead in July

Routes & Navigation

  • Meuse RAVeL (EuroVelo 19) — Official GPX tracks at ravel.wallonie.be and eurovelo.com
  • EuroVelo 5 (Dinant → La Roche section) — eurovelo.com
  • RAVeL 163 (Bastogne → Gouvy) — ravel.wallonie.be; search “RAVeL 163”
  • Vennbahn — Official GPX and route info at vennbahn.eu; well-signposted with dedicated markers

Weather

  • July is peak season — expect warm days (20–27°C) with possible afternoon thunderstorms in the Ardennes
  • The High Fens moorland can be cold and foggy even in summer; pack a wind layer for Day 4
  • Summer days are long (light until 10pm) — useful for the bigger mileage days

Route Highlights

Meuse Valley

Namur Citadel

The largest medieval fortress in Belgium, rebuilt by Vauban in the 17th century on a rocky spur above the confluence of the Meuse and Sambre rivers. Extensive underground tunnels and panoramic views over the city. Cable car or walking path from the old town.

Jardins d’Annevoie

One of the most remarkable gardens in Belgium — 18th-century formal gardens where all the fountains, cascades, and water features run entirely on gravity, with no pumps. The water arrives from natural springs at different heights and is routed to create over 40 distinct water features. Best visited in summer when everything is in full flow.

Maredsous Abbey

Neo-Gothic Benedictine monastery founded in 1872, built dramatically into a hillside in the Molignée valley. The monks still brew Maredsous 6, 8, and 10 and produce cheese on-site. The abbey shop and café are open to visitors; drinking a Maredsous 10 in the courtyard is one of the route highlights.

Dinant

The most photographed view in Wallonia: a collegiate church with a distinctive onion dome, a cliff-top citadel, and the Meuse reflected below, all squeezed into a narrow gorge. Birthplace of Adolphe Sax (inventor of the saxophone) in 1814, and of Leffe beer, brewed at the nearby abbey since 1152.

Ardennes Interior

Rochefort Trappist Brewery

One of 14 authentic Trappist breweries worldwide, producing beer since 1595 at the Abbaye Notre-Dame de Saint-Rémy. The Rochefort 10 (11.3% ABV, dark quadrupel) is consistently rated among the world’s greatest beers. The brewery is not open for tours, but the abbey church can be visited and every café in town serves all three Rochefort varieties on tap.

La Roche-en-Ardenne

The capital of Ardennes cycling. A small town where the Ourthe river loops in a tight meander, surrounded on all sides by steep forested hills with a ruined 9th-century castle on the hilltop. This is the hub that most Belgian and Dutch cycling holidays use as a base. The town is touristic but the surrounding riding is exceptional.

Hérou Viewpoint (Nadrin)

Widely considered the most dramatic viewpoint in the Belgian Ardennes. From a promontory above Nadrin, you look down several hundred meters to see the Ourthe river looping through seven visible meanders, surrounded by unbroken forest as far as the eye can see. A 10-minute hike from the parking area.

Bastogne War Museum

Modern museum (opened 2014) covering the Battle of the Bulge — the largest land battle in American military history, fought in the Ardennes during the winter of 1944–45. The museum uses an immersive format where you follow one of four characters (American soldier, German soldier, Belgian civilian, Belgian resistance fighter) through the battle. Allow 2 hours minimum.

RAVeL 163

The Wallonian RAVeL (Réseau Autonome de Voies Lentes) network converts former railway and canal towpaths to cycling routes. Line 163 runs east from Bastogne through the deep Ardennes forest, crossing viaducts and running through rock cuttings on a former military railway. Cited by cyclists as among the most scenic trails in the entire RAVeL network.

Vennbahn & High Fens

The Vennbahn

Europe’s longest cross-border rail trail — 125km converted from a Prussian military railway built in 1889 to supply troops in the Eifel. After World War I, the Treaty of Versailles transferred the railway to Belgium but left the German towns on either side German, creating a bizarre situation where the trail passes through a 3m-wide strip of Belgian territory entirely surrounded by Germany. Max gradient 2%, awarded Cycling Route of the Year 2014.

Burg-Reuland Castle

A 10th-century castle ruin on a rocky promontory above the Our river valley, in one of the most scenic sections of the Vennbahn. The nearby Lengeler tunnel has been converted into a registered bat habitat; cycling through it and hearing the bats is a memorable experience.

High Fens (Hautes Fagnes)

Belgium’s largest nature reserve, covering 700km² of wild peatland moorland on the plateau between the Ardennes and the Eifel. Signal de Botrange (694m) is Belgium’s highest point. The landscape — vast open bog with wooden boardwalks, cotton grass, and cloudberry — feels entirely unlike the rest of Belgium, more reminiscent of the Scottish Highlands or Scandinavian fell. Protected since 1971.

Monschau

A German market town from the 17th century, squeezed into a narrow gorge of the Rur river. Half-timbered buildings in mustard yellow and deep red climb the steep valley sides; the 17th-century Red House (Rotes Haus), built by a cloth merchant dynasty, is the most famous. Rated 4.7 stars with over 9,000 reviews; regularly listed among Germany’s most beautiful small towns. The Christmas market (December) is famous across Germany, but the town is wonderful in summer too.

Aachen

Aachen Cathedral (Dom)

Germany’s first UNESCO World Heritage Site (listed 1978) and one of the most historically significant buildings in Europe. Charlemagne built his Palatine Chapel here in 796–805, modelled on the Byzantine San Vitale in Ravenna. For nearly 600 years it was the coronation church of the Holy Roman Emperors — 30 kings and 12 queens were crowned here. The octagonal central space, with its original 9th-century mosaics, is extraordinarily preserved. Charlemagne’s marble throne sits in the upper gallery. The Gothic choir (14th century) contains his gold reliquary shrine. €7 guided tour strongly recommended — the treasury next door holds priceless medieval artifacts including his skull reliquary.

Aachener Printen

Aachen’s famous spiced gingerbread-like cookies, with protected geographical indication. Harder and spicier than typical gingerbread, with a distinct flavor from beet syrup and a mixture of spices including anise, cardamom, and cloves. Available year-round at Aachen confectioners — Leo Driessen and Nobis Printen are the most famous makers.
